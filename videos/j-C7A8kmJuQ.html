This 10¢ Battery Turns Seawater Into Freshwater

@octopusinc_ · · Hardware

Seawater battery by Professor Kim Youngsik (UNIST, Korea) turns ocean water into freshwater while charging and pulls CO2 from air while discharging. Uses ion-selective NASICON membranes to separate sodium/chloride, achieving ~40% energy savings vs reverse osmosis; cathode costs only 10¢/kWh vs $78 for lithium-ion. Battery TRL 5–6; desalination/carbon capture TRL 3–4.

How it works: seawater enters, sodium and chloride ions fork into separate pathways (not both stored in cathode). Separation mechanism uses ion-selective NASICON membranes (ceramic, sodium super ion conductor)—only sodium passes through; acts as chemical bouncer. Sodium passes through NASICON into anhydrous (water-free) compartment where it plates as pure metal (charge separation of battery). Chloride filtered separately via different membrane (69–85% passes through, held back by salt dress code). Result: seawater in → freshwater + brine out. Energy savings: same amount NaCl removed costs 2.51 Wh (seawater battery) vs 4.06 Wh (reverse osmosis)—38% less energy. Because battery charges directly vs high-pressure pumps. Caveat: seawater is 80% NaCl; remaining 20% (magnesium, calcium, others) not removed; output is brackish, needs polishing reverse osmosis to reach drinkable purity (but RO on brackish uses far less energy than RO on seawater). Carbon capture (discharge phase): sodium metal converts back to ions; alkaline water (from oxygen reaction at positive electrode) absorbs CO₂ from bubbled air; carbonates precipitate as powder, locking carbon away.
