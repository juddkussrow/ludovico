Transparent Tech (90s–2000s consumer electronics): translucent/clear plastic (lime green, purple, icy blue, orange), visible internal components. Use: tech branding, product packaging, app icons, print-on-demand. 2. Memphis Design (1980s, Milan-based collective): flat geometric shapes (triangles, zigzags, dots, squiggles), layered without gradients/shadows, primary colors (red, yellow, blue) + secondary (pink, teal, acid green), black outlines, intentionally busy compositions. Use: event branding, posters, apparel, high-energy branding. 3. Akira Core (80s/90s Japanese cyberpunk): dark backgrounds, bright reds/pinks/blues/sharp yellows, Japanese typography (kanji, katakana), cinematic framing, motion lines, technology-vs-humanity tension. Use: apparel, music/event branding, gaming, editorial, album covers. 4. 90s Editorial (1988–1999 print advertising): large white space, single product cutout, clean type hierarchy, minimal copy, black/white/one-two colors, condensed sans-serif or strong serif. Use: brand identity, tech branding, editorial, confident messaging. 5. Neon Synthwave (1980s retrofuturism): deep purple/black, neon pink/teal, warm gradients, chrome/neon text, perspective grids, palm silhouettes, glow/lens effects, layered depth. Use: music branding, event posters, gaming, apparel. 6. 8-Bit Pixel Art (early video game consoles): visible pixel grid, 4–16 flat colors, no gradients, no sub-pixel detail. Examples: Space Invaders, Pac-Man, Nintendo. Use: gaming brands, retro apparel, POD, mascots, icon design. 7. Airbrush Illustration (1970s–1980s commercial art): smooth gradients, hyperrealistic sheen, glossy 3D quality, product focus (drinks, sneakers, tech, packaging), gradient backgrounds, strong highlights/shadows. Use: product packaging, posters, retro branding. 8. Pop Art (1950s–1960s): Benday halftone dots, black outlines, flat primary fills, high contrast, Roy Lichtenstein / Andy Warhol references, text as visual element (POW, BOOM, speech bubbles). Use: posters, apparel, packaging, campaign branding. 9. Risograph/Risoprint (Japanese stencil printer, 1980s): one color at a time, slight misregistration/offset, porous grainy texture, 2–4 colors max, unexpected color pairings (fluorescent pink, teal, yellow, navy), faded/washed quality. Use: event posters, music packaging, zines, greeting cards, indie/hand-crafted signaling. 10. Functional Utilitarian (Severance design/Dieter Rams, 1950s–1980s): strict grids, neutral sans-serif, high contrast, black/white/gray + restrained accent color (blue, pink, muted green), every element placed for purpose, no decoration. Use: tech/product branding, corporate identity, packaging, intelligent positioning. Searchable search terms provided for Pinterest, Google, Instagram.
