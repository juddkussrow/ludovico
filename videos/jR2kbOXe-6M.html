Machine: Molecular Beam Epitaxy (MBE) operates in high vacuum (10^-10 Torr). Process: Heat metal sources (barium, strontium, titanium, oxygen) in crucibles. Metals evaporate and travel upward to a silicon wafer substrate held at the chamber center, facing downward into the metal flux. Chamber walls cooled to -10°C (via coolant circulation) so evaporated metals condense on walls instead of bouncing back to the wafer, preserving film purity. Film thickness: few nanometers to 500 nanometers (not millimeters). Material is 3-element compound (BTO has barium, titanium, oxygen; STO has strontium, titanium, oxygen). Ratio control between three elements is critical—poor stoichiometry degrades single-photon quantum computer performance. Process is intentionally slow (cannot rush) to avoid crystalline defects. Clean room: Class 1000 (1,000 particles/ft³ max) for initial work; Class 100 (100 particles/ft³) target for high-volume manufacturing. Personnel in full-body clean suits to prevent airborne contamination. Rare metal handling: BTO/STO metals are hazardous if exposed; surface oxide forms instantly in air so material is stored sealed. Workers wear full protective gear when handling crucibles. Automated transfer: Finished wafers move in sealed containers via automation from MBE to automated storage—no manual exposure. Inspection: Electron-beam imaging reveals crystal lattice dendrite patterns. Color variation in finished wafers indicates interference (different light wavelengths reflected based on film thickness and refractive index). Shipping: FedEx wafers to GlobalFoundries for further integration into silicon photonics stack for FTQC applications. Cost and timeline not disclosed.
